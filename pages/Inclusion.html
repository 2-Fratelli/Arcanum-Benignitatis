People with disabilities

For people who have a condition that requires accommodations (such as a wheelchair, hearing aid, guide dog, or other support) to move through society safely and comfortably, their disability may become a central part of their identity. This is because it is often the aspect of themselves that faces the most discrimination or exclusion, and it usually requires the greatest effort to manage in everyday interactions with others.

The term “disability” is defined by the federal government in several ways, but depends on the context.

For the purposes of federal disability nondiscrimination laws, the definition of a person with a disability is typically defined as someone who “has a physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities, has a record of such an impairment, or is regarded as having such an impairment.”

Myth vs Reality

Myth Reality
People with disabilities are inferior to “normal” people and their lives are very different. What is “normal”?People with disabilities go to school, work, have families, play, do laundry, go shopping, eat out, travel, vote, pay taxes, laugh, cry, plan and dream. Just like everyone else.
We need to feel sorry for people with disabilities. That’s condescending. People with disabilities don’t need pity. They need, as everyone, the same opportunities.
You have to be careful when you’re talking to a person with a disability, because you can offend them easily. You just need to be as polite and respectful as you would when speaking to anyone. If you’re not sure what to say or do, it’s okay to ask.
